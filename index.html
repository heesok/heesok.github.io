I am a second-year PhD student in the Program in Neural Computation at Carnegie Mellon University, where I work with Jenelle Feather and Abigail Noyce. My research focuses on understanding how humans process sounds in the brain and how computational models can help us better understand human auditory perception. In particular, I study invariances in auditory event categorization, like how we are able to recognize a sound as belonging to a category, such as a “dog bark”, despite large variability across individual instances. My work combines behavioral experiments, brain imaging, and computational modeling to investigate how the brain represents and generalizes across complex natural sounds.

Prior to starting my PhD, I studied neuroscience at Wellesley College. I also worked as a undergraduate researcher and later as a full-time research assistant in Ev Fedorenko’s lab at MIT, where I studied prosody processing in the brain.

I am also passionate about mentorship and broadening access to science. I have helped teach introductory neuroscience workshops for elementary and middle school students (Gelfand Center) and mentored students who have had limited access to research opportunities (Sci-MI).
